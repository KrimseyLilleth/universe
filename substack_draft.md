# The Shape of Everything
## A geometric formula for spacetime — tested against 12 objects across the observable universe.

What if the universe is a torus?

Not a metaphor. Not a thought experiment. A literal, geometric torus — like a donut — where everything we observe sits on a single cross-sectional slice, and the math checks out against real astronomical data.

That's the claim I'm making in a paper I recently published. So let me show you the math.

---

### The relationship

Take two numbers that cosmologists have measured extensively:

- The **diameter of the observable universe**: about 93 billion light-years
- The **age of the universe**: about 13.8 billion years

Now take **φ** — the golden ratio, 1.618... — a number that shows up everywhere in nature, from the spiral of a nautilus shell to the branching of trees to the arrangement of seeds in a sunflower. Raise it to the fourth power:

φ⁴ ≈ 6.854

Now multiply:

13.8 billion × 6.854 ≈ 94.5 billion light-years

That's within 1.6% of the measured diameter.

No tuning. No adjustable knobs. Just one number — the golden ratio — connecting the size of the universe to its age.

**D = T × φ⁴**

This is the formula. And here's what makes it unusual: it doesn't depend on the Hubble constant.

The Hubble constant is the number cosmologists use to describe how fast the universe is expanding. Nearly every calculation in modern cosmology runs through it — it's how scientists convert what they observe (light from distant galaxies) into physical quantities like distance, size, and age. It's the conversion factor at the center of the whole operation.

The problem is, right now, nobody agrees on what it actually is. There's a famous disagreement in physics — called the "Hubble tension" — where two different ways of measuring it give two different answers, and no one knows why. This matters because if your conversion factor is uncertain, so is everything downstream of it.

The relationship above sidesteps that problem entirely. It connects size and age directly through geometry — no expansion rate, no conversion factor, no Hubble constant required.

That could be a coincidence. But I followed the thread.

### The model

If you take that relationship seriously — if the ratio between the universe's size and age is genuinely governed by φ⁴ — it implies a specific shape. That shape is a torus: a closed, curved structure where space flows along a loop, expanding from a pinch point to a maximum width and then contracting back.

Think of it this way:

- The **spine** of the torus is the path that time travels along — a circle
- The **cross-section** grows outward from the spine as time moves forward — small and dense near the beginning, expanding as the universe ages
- It reaches a **maximum** — the widest point — and then contracts back down
- The model places that maximum at 2012. Oddly — or maybe not so oddly — that's the year the Mayan calendar marked as the Great Turning, the end of a 5,125-year cycle
- At that widest point, the cross-section is a sphere. That sphere is the observable universe as it exists right now. Everything we can see lives inside it.

### Testing it against reality

A formula connecting size and age is interesting. But the real question is: does the geometry predict anything else?

To test this, I derived a formula for something called **comoving distance**.

Here's what that means. When astronomers say a galaxy is "10 billion light-years away," they don't mean it took light 10 billion years to reach us. The universe has been expanding the whole time the light was traveling, so the galaxy is actually much farther away now than it was when the light left. Comoving distance is the "true" distance — where things actually are right now, accounting for all that expansion.

[PLACEHOLDER: Diagram or image illustrating comoving distance vs. light travel distance]

Calculating comoving distances is one of the core tasks in cosmology. The standard approach uses a model with six numbers that are carefully fitted to match observations. Change any of those numbers and the predictions shift.

The torus model produces a comoving distance formula where every constant comes from φ — the golden ratio:

**d = R × [ A × (1 - x^(1/φ²)) - (1/φ⁴) × (1 - x) ]**

Where x is just how far back in time you're looking, expressed as a fraction of the universe's age. The constant A is set by one boundary condition: the known distance to the cosmic microwave background — the oldest light in the universe. That's it. One anchor point.

The formula then predicts the distances to everything else, with no further adjustment.

I tested it against 12 observed objects spanning nearly the entire observable universe — from the Sloan Great Wall (relatively nearby) all the way to the cosmic microwave background (the edge of the observable universe, from when the cosmos was only 380,000 years old):

| Object | Observed Distance | Predicted Distance | Error |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sloan Great Wall | 1.08 Bly | 1.09 Bly | +0.9% |
| 3C 273 (quasar) | 2.08 Bly | 2.05 Bly | −1.5% |
| BOSS Great Wall | 5.87 Bly | 5.66 Bly | −3.6% |
| El Gordo Cluster | 9.56 Bly | 9.51 Bly | −0.5% |
| GN-z11 (early galaxy) | 32.0 Bly | 31.84 Bly | −0.5% |
| CMB (oldest light) | 46.0 Bly | 46.37 Bly | +0.8% |

*(Six of twelve shown — full table in the [paper](https://zenodo.org/records/18438694))*

**Average error across all 12 objects: 2.66%.**

One formula. One anchor point. The golden ratio does the rest.

### The contraction prediction

If the model is right, the universe hit peak expansion around 2012 and is now — very slowly — beginning to contract. That's a bold claim.

But in March 2025, the DESI collaboration — one of the largest cosmological survey projects in the world — released results suggesting that dark energy may be weakening over time, not holding steady as the standard model assumes.

Then in November 2025, a study by Yonsei University went further. Combining corrected supernova data with DESI observations, the researchers concluded that "the universe has already entered a phase of decelerated expansion at the present epoch." Their analysis ruled out the standard constant-dark-energy model "with overwhelming significance."

This torus model predicts that independently, from pure geometry.

This remains an active area of debate — but the geometry and the observations are pointing in the same direction.

So I'm sharing it, one piece at a time, starting with the piece that's hardest to dismiss.
